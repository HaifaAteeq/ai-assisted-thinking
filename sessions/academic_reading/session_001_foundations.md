# Academic Reading & Writing
## Session 001: What Is A Paper Review and How Do Researchers Read?

---

## Session Plan

**Goal:** Ground the skill of academic reading before practicing it

**Duration:** 45-60 minutes

**Energy Required:** 3+ recommended

**Prerequisites:** None - this is the foundation

---

## Session Script

### Opening (Energy Check)

```
AI: "Today we're building a foundation for academic reading and writing.

Before we start:
1. Energy level? (1=tired, 5=sharp)
2. Time available?
3. Have you read academic papers before? (Never / A few / Many)"
```

---

### Part 1: Grounding - What Is A Paper Review? (10 min)

#### Connect to Experience

```
AI: "Before we talk about paper reviews, let me ask:

Have you ever watched a movie and told a friend:
- What it was about
- Whether it was good or bad
- Whether they should watch it?

What did you include when you told them?"
```

**Wait for response.**

```
AI: "That's basically a review. You:
- Summarized (what happened)
- Evaluated (good or bad, and why)
- Recommended (should they watch)

A paper review is the same thing:
- Summary: What problem did they solve? How? What did they find?
- Evaluation: What's strong? What's weak?
- Recommendation: Should others read this?

Same skill, more structure."
```

#### Verification

```
AI: "Explain back to me:
What are the three parts of a paper review?"
```

**Expected answer:** Summary, evaluation, recommendation (in their own words)

---

### Part 2: Why Do Researchers Write Reviews? (5 min)

```
AI: "Why do you think researchers write reviews of papers?

Not 'because professor told them to'—what's the real purpose?"
```

**Wait for response. Guide toward:**

```
AI: "Paper reviews serve several purposes:

1. For YOU: Forces you to actually understand, not just skim
2. For OTHERS: Helps them decide if paper is worth reading
3. For FIELD: Quality control—identifies strong and weak work
4. For AUTHORS: Feedback to improve their research

When you write a review, you're not just summarizing.
You're thinking critically: Is this good science?"
```

#### Verification

```
AI: "If someone just reads a paper but doesn't write a review,
what might they miss?"
```

**Looking for:** Understanding that writing forces deeper processing

---

### Part 3: How Researchers Actually Read (15 min)

#### The Problem with Word-by-Word

```
AI: "How do you currently read a paper?
Be honest—do you start at the beginning and read every word?"
```

**Wait for response.**

```
AI: "Most people read word-by-word. Here's the problem:

Academic papers are NOT written to be read top to bottom.
They're written to be read STRATEGICALLY.

If you read word-by-word:
- Takes 2-3 hours per paper
- You get lost in details
- You miss the big picture
- You forget by the end what the beginning said

Researchers don't read this way."
```

#### The Three-Pass Method

```
AI: "Experienced researchers use something like this:

PASS 1: Bird's Eye View (5 minutes)
- Read ONLY: title, abstract, conclusion
- Look at figures and tables
- Answer: 'What is this paper about?'
- Decision: Is it worth reading more?

PASS 2: Structure (20 minutes)
- Read: introduction, method headings, results summary
- Skip: detailed math, proofs, dense paragraphs
- Answer: 'What did they do and find?'
- Decision: Do I need the details?

PASS 3: Deep Dive (30+ minutes)
- Read everything carefully
- Take notes
- Answer: 'Why did they do it this way? What's missing?'
- Only for papers you really need to understand

Most papers only need Pass 1.
Some need Pass 2.
Few need Pass 3."
```

#### Verification

```
AI: "Quick check:

1. In Pass 1, what parts do you read?
2. How long should Pass 1 take?
3. What question are you trying to answer in Pass 1?
4. Why don't you read the Method section in Pass 1?"
```

**Looking for:**
1. Title, abstract, conclusion, figures
2. 5 minutes
3. "What is this paper about?"
4. Because it's detailed/you're just getting overview

---

### Part 4: Paper Structure (10 min)

```
AI: "Before you can read strategically, you need to know what each section does.

ABSTRACT
- One paragraph summary of EVERYTHING
- The 'movie trailer' of the paper
- Read this first always

INTRODUCTION
- What problem are they solving?
- Why does it matter?
- What's their approach?
- Read this to understand the WHY

RELATED WORK
- What have others done?
- How is this paper different?
- Can skim or skip unless you need context

METHOD
- HOW did they do it?
- Technical details, algorithms, data
- Read carefully only if you need to replicate or deeply understand

RESULTS
- WHAT did they find?
- Look at figures and tables first
- Numbers and evidence

DISCUSSION
- What do results mean?
- Limitations
- Future work

CONCLUSION
- Summary of everything
- Read in Pass 1 along with abstract"
```

#### Verification

```
AI: "Match each question to the section where you'd find the answer:

1. 'How big was their dataset?' 
2. 'Why is this problem important?'
3. 'What's the main finding in one sentence?'
4. 'What algorithm did they use?'
5. 'What are the limitations?'

Sections: Abstract, Introduction, Method, Results, Discussion"
```

**Answers:**
1. Method or Results
2. Introduction
3. Abstract or Conclusion
4. Method
5. Discussion

---

### Part 5: Hands-On Practice - Pass 1 (15 min)

```
AI: "Let's practice Pass 1 with a real paper.

Option A: Use a paper you need to read anyway
Option B: I'll suggest a simple paper

Which do you prefer?"
```

**If they have a paper:**
```
AI: "Perfect. Open it now.

DO NOT read the whole thing. Only:
1. Title
2. Abstract
3. Conclusion (last paragraph)
4. Glance at figures

Set a timer for 5 minutes. Tell me when done."
```

**After 5 minutes:**
```
AI: "Based on ONLY what you just read, tell me:

1. What problem are they solving? (One sentence, your words)
2. What did they do? (One sentence, your words)
3. What did they find? (One sentence, your words)

Don't copy the abstract. Pretend you're telling a friend."
```

**Evaluate response:**
- If copies abstract → "That's the paper's words. How would YOU explain it?"
- If vague → "Be more specific. What EXACTLY is the problem?"
- If clear → "Good! That's Pass 1. In 5 minutes you know what this paper is about."

## Common Stuck Points

| Issue | Response |
|-------|----------|
| "I need to read everything to understand" | "Try Pass 1 first. You'll be surprised how much you get in 5 minutes." |
| Copies abstract word-for-word | "Those are their words. Pretend you're texting a friend—how would you say it?" |
| Summary too vague | "What SPECIFICALLY is the problem? Give me one concrete detail." |
| Overwhelmed by paper | "Ignore everything except title, abstract, conclusion. Just those three." |
| "I don't understand the abstract" | "That's okay. What DO you understand? Even one word or concept?" |
